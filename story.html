Environment: Nature Positive

Nine Years
of Gold

At a car factory in West Virginia, 90% of the grounds are now managed for pollinators. This is the story of what happened when Toyota asked what the land wanted to be.

Maya Osei-Bonsu
Environmental Programmes, Toyota Newsroom
Environment · Nature Positive
6 min read
Source: Toyota Newsroom / Environmental Site
Share

The Toyota Motor Manufacturing plant in Buffalo, West Virginia, builds engines. It also keeps bees. It maintains a wildflower meadow, manages native plantings across more than a dozen distinct habitat projects, and runs environmental education visits for local schoolchildren. In 2024, the Wildlife Habitat Council recognised it with Gold certification for the ninth consecutive year. Ninety percent of the plant's green space has now been converted into functioning habitat for pollinators and native wildlife. The factory is, by almost any measure, one of the most ecologically productive industrial sites in North America.

This did not happen by accident. It happened because, in 1999, Toyota Motor North America made a decision: that its plants and business sites should do more than occupy land. They should improve it. A partnership was established with Tandem Global, an international non-profit organisation working on biodiversity conservation across 19 countries. A programme was built. Sites were assessed. Native species were planted. Habitats were established, monitored, and refined. Twenty-five years later, 17 Toyota sites across North America hold Wildlife Habitat Council certification, at Gold, Silver, and Certified levels.

The West Virginia plant is the flagship. But the ambition behind it is now written into Toyota North America's 8th Environmental Action Plan as a formal commitment: protect or restore 62,000 acres of land, inland water, and marine and coastal ecosystems across the region.

"

Protecting and enhancing habitats for pollinators like the Monarch Butterfly is not a side project. It is part of how we understand our responsibility to the places where we operate.

Becky Martin Biodiversity Focus Group Lead, Toyota Motor North America

What Ninety Percent Looks Like

The number is striking when you sit with it. Not 20%. Not half. Ninety percent of a working car factory's available green space managed as active wildlife habitat. That means native grasses and wildflowers where there were once maintained lawns. Beehives tended by team members trained in apiculture. Nesting structures for solitary bees. Habitat corridors connecting planting areas to maximise ecological value rather than treating each project in isolation. And a Biodiversity Park within the plant grounds that is open to the public, used for school visits and community recreation.

The WHC Ibis Award, which Toyota West Virginia received in 2022, is given specifically to projects that have sustained biodiversity work under difficult conditions over time. It is not a prize for ambition. It is a prize for persistence and follow-through, the recognition that habitat is built over years, not quarters, and that the organisations who do this well are those who treat it as a permanent commitment rather than a communications cycle.

The Science of Pollinator Habitat

The pollinators at the centre of Toyota's habitat work are not simply photogenic. They are functionally critical. Bees, butterflies, beetles, and hoverflies are responsible for the pollination of approximately 75% of global food crops. The Monarch Butterfly, specifically called out in Toyota North America's environmental planning, has lost more than 80% of its population over the past two decades, largely because the milkweed it depends on has disappeared from the agricultural and industrial landscapes of North America. Re-establishing that milkweed, alongside the nectar sources that support the full range of pollinator species, is the practical work that Toyota's habitat teams do at sites from Kentucky to Mississippi to Oregon.

The approach is grounded in ecology rather than aesthetics. Toyota works with Tandem Global and site-specific ecological consultants to identify the native plant communities that belong in each location: the grasses, wildflowers, shrubs, and sedges that evolved alongside local insect populations over millennia. A meadow of non-native ornamentals provides almost no ecological value. A meadow of the right native species can support hundreds of invertebrate species, provide nesting habitat for ground-nesting bees, and create the food webs that sustain birds and mammals up the chain. Species selection is the work. Getting it right takes time and expertise and monitoring.

Water: The Hidden Multiplier

Alongside biodiversity, water is the second dimension of Toyota's Nature Positive commitment. Reducing water withdrawals, managing water quality, and understanding the true cost of water across manufacturing operations are all part of the 8th Environmental Action Plan. A cost-of-water study completed across Toyota North America facilities in 2025 and 2026 found something striking: the full cost of water, once treatment, wastewater, and operational costs are factored in, is three to five times higher than the price paid to the water utility. That changes the economics of water reduction projects significantly, reducing payback periods by years rather than months.

The ecological and the financial turn out to reinforce each other. Replacing impermeable surfaces with rain gardens and native plantings reduces stormwater runoff, improves water quality in adjacent watercourses, and creates wetland habitats that support dragonflies, amphibians, and the birds that feed on them. These are not incidental outcomes. They were designed for. And they are built into how Toyota accounts for the true cost of its operations.

Once you understand the full cost of water, not just what you pay the utility but what it actually costs to treat, use, and discharge it, the case for reduction becomes very different. A Park That Belongs to Everyone

The detail that stays with you about the West Virginia programme is not the Gold certification, impressive as nine consecutive years is. It is that the Biodiversity Park is open to the public. Local families use it for walks. Schoolchildren visit for environmental education sessions. Community groups come to learn about native plants and pollinators. A car factory has become, quietly and deliberately, a piece of public green infrastructure in the community it operates within.

This is what "Best in Town" means in practice, the phrase Toyota uses to describe its ambition for its relationship with local communities. Not just employment and tax revenue. Not just compliance and community grants. But a physical presence in the landscape that leaves the place genuinely better for the people and species who share it. Toyota team members tend the hives, volunteer on planting days, and walk past the meadows they helped establish every working day of their lives. The Monarch Butterflies, when they pass through on their annual migration, stop here because someone made sure there was somewhere for them to stop.

Toyota's stated purpose is producing happiness for all. It is easy to read that phrase at the scale of vehicles and mobility and global ambition. At the West Virginia plant, it is also 90% of a factory's grounds given back to nature. It is a child on a school trip watching a bee work a wildflower that her parents' generation did not plant here, because it did not exist here. It is a commitment, twenty-five years old and still building, to the idea that a company and the land it occupies can be genuinely good for each other.

How Nature Positive fits Toyota's broader environmental strategy
Relationship to Carbon Neutrality and Circular Economy pillars
▾

Nature Positive is one of Toyota's three environmental pillars alongside Carbon Neutrality and Circular Economy. The three are deeply complementary. Carbon neutrality addresses what goes into the atmosphere. Circular economy addresses materials and resources. Nature Positive addresses the living systems that both shape and are shaped by Toyota's presence in the world. In practice the pillars interact: restored habitat captures and stores carbon. Native meadows support pollinators essential to agricultural food systems. Circular economy initiatives that reduce waste also reduce pressure on the ecosystems that provide raw materials. Toyota's integrated approach treats these as dimensions of a single commitment, not separate programmes with separate targets.

Toyota measures Nature Positive progress against confirmed targets in the 8th Environmental Action Plan, including the 62,000 acres protection and restoration target for North America, Wildlife Habitat Council certification levels across 17 North American sites, and water withdrawal reduction metrics at priority facilities. The full cost-of-water study, completed across four plants in FY2026, is used to prioritise and size water reduction investments, with the finding that full water costs are three to five times the utility price.
